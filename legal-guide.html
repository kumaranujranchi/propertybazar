Legal Guide: Buying Property in India

Comprehensive checklist and essential steps to safeguard your property investment with rigorous legal due diligence.

Buying property in India requires rigorous legal due diligence. Key checks include verifying ownership records, ensuring all statutory approvals are in place, and understanding transfer charges. Follow these steps to safeguard your investment:

Government Land Records & Title Verification

Check Online Land Records

Every state has a land-records portal (e.g. Andhra Pradesh's Meebhoomi, Karnataka's Bhoomi, Maharashtra's Bhulekh, Punjab's PLRS, etc.) where you can view ownership details, survey (khasra) numbers, and mutation status. Use the portal to confirm the seller's name, plot area, and any attached liabilities. For example, Housing.com notes steps to view Record of Rights (RoR), khatauni, and mutation history online.

Encumbrance Certificate (EC)

Obtain an EC from the local Sub-Registrar's office for the past 10–30 years. This certificate lists all registered transactions on the property, showing if there are mortgages, liens or legal claims. A 'Nil EC' (Form 16) indicates the property is free of encumbrances. Many states now allow online EC applications, but you can also apply in person.

Title Search

Use state registration portals to conduct a title search by registration number or owner name. For example, Karnataka's Bhoomi portal or Maharashtra's e-search (Mahabhulekh) let you view prior sale deeds. The MagicBricks guide lists portals for each state, which helps verify that the chain of ownership is clear.

Required Documents & Statutory Clearances

Before finalizing the purchase, collect and verify all essential documents:

Title Deed(s)

Obtain the original sale deed from the seller, along with the chain of previous deeds ("mother deeds") showing uninterrupted title. Ensure the seller's name exactly matches the title and there are no pending transfers.

Land Conversion Certificate

If the plot was classified as agricultural, it must have been legally converted to residential/commercial use. A Land Conversion Certificate from the local authority proves the change of land use. Without it, building approvals and utilities may be denied later.

Approved Plans & Occupancy

For a built property, check that the building plan was approved by the municipality or local body. Also obtain the Completion Certificate (CC) and Occupancy Certificate (OC) to confirm legal construction. Without OC, lenders won't approve home loans.

Property Tax Receipts

Collect the latest 3–5 years of tax receipts or khata chit (in Karnataka) to ensure all dues are paid. Unpaid taxes can become your liability.

Khata / Patta / Adangal

Depending on the state, this is the local land register entry for the property. For example, Bangalore uses khata (RTC), Tamil Nadu uses patta & chitta. Verify these documents to confirm the property is in municipal records.

Power of Attorney / Consent

If the seller used a POA or if multiple owners exist, ensure valid notarized POAs or consents from all co-owners (including NRI, minor, or absent owners). If the property is inherited, verify succession certificates or mutation records as needed.

Builder and Society NOCs

For flats or township projects, obtain a No-Objection Certificate from the builder or co-operative society. Also get the society's registration certificates and share certificates. Check the developer's credentials and any RERA registration for new projects.

Encroachment / Boundary Verification

Physically inspect the site and compare with survey maps or cadastral plans. Look out for encroachments by neighbors. Ensure the boundaries in documents match the actual site.

Additional Clearances

Depending on location, you may need other clearances (e.g., environmental clearance, hazard NOC if near highways, approval for septic tanks). Confirm no breaches of local zoning laws or FAR/FSI norms.

Stamp Duty, Registration & Mutation

Stamp Duty & Registration Fees

The buyer must pay stamp duty to the state (a tax on the sale deed) and a registration fee at the sub-registrar's office. Stamp duty rates vary by state (typically 3–10% of the property value) and often include rebates (e.g. for women buyers). Registration fee is usually a flat ~1% of value. Payment is mandatory under the Indian Registration Act 1908.

Sale Deed Registration

Ensure the sale deed is prepared on stamp paper and registered in person by both buyer and seller at the local sub-registrar. Registration officially transfers title and creates a public record. Keep certified copies of the registered deed for your records.

Mutation of Property

After registration, immediately file for mutation (entry of new ownership) in the revenue or municipal records. Mutation updates the local Khata or RoR with the buyer's name and is needed to pay future property taxes. File the mutation application with the new sale deed, EC, tax receipts, and ID proof. Only a mutated record grants full legal recognition of ownership.

Common Red Flags & Due Diligence Tips

Watch for these warning signs during due diligence:

Unclear Title

Title deeds should be chain-linked. Any missing documents or gaps in ownership history are dangerous. Verify that the seller is the absolute owner and there are no undisclosed co-owners.

Pending Litigation

Check if the property is subject to any court cases (search district court records or ask the seller). Any ongoing title disputes or partition suits should be resolved before purchase.

Outstanding Encumbrances

A non-Nil EC means existing mortgages or liens. Identify any banks or third parties with claims and ensure those loans are paid off by the seller at closing.

Illegal Constructions

Unauthorized structures (built without permits or beyond approved plan) can lead to demolitions or fines. Verify all building works comply with local laws.

Agricultural Land Misuse

If the seller bought agricultural land and then illegally plotted or sold it as residential without conversion, that is invalid. Always have conversion approvals for non-agrarian use.

Discrepancies in Documents

Ensure that the names, addresses, and identification in all documents (deeds, tax records, POAs) match exactly. Watch for forged signatures or antiquated documents.

High Development Charges

In some jurisdictions, purchasing a converted plot may still incur development charges. Check whether additional stamp duty (e.g. agricultural cess) applies.

Multiple Sales / Mortgage

Beware of properties offered by several parties or already mortgaged. Encumbrance search can catch this. Also look for "benami" transactions (sold secretly).

Neighborhood Restrictions

Confirm that the intended use of property is permitted (e.g. some societies disallow ground-floor sale, or gated communities have sub-leasing rules).

Professional Contacts & Dispute Resolution

Legal Advisors

Engage a qualified property lawyer or legal consultant to verify documents, draft agreements, and handle registration. Law firms specializing in real-estate due diligence can catch hidden issues.

Surveyor

A land surveyor or civil engineer can verify boundaries and constructions against approved plans.

Local Authorities

The Sub-Registrar, Tehsildar, and Municipal Corporation offices can provide official records and clarify procedures.

RERA Authorities

For apartment projects or plotted developments, the State Real Estate Regulatory Authority (RERA) can resolve builder-buyer disputes. Check the project's RERA registration and any complaints filed.

Consumer Forums

Homebuyers have recourse to Consumer Courts for deficiency in service by developers or realtors. For example, courts have held that once a complaint is filed under RERA, a civil remedy may be limited.

Alternative Dispute Resolution

In contentious cases (fraud, unpaid dues, title suits), parties can pursue civil litigation in civil courts or invoke arbitration/mediation clauses if present. Title insurance (though still nascent in India) is another emerging way to protect against hidden title risks.

Key Takeaways

  • Verify ownership through official records and online portals in your state
  • Ensure all approvals are in place (building plans, occupancy cert, conversion cert if needed)
  • Pay required taxes and duties (stamp duty, registration fee) under law
  • Update government records via mutation after purchase
  • Engage experts (lawyers, surveyors) to catch hidden risks
  • Watch for red flags (unclear title, pending litigation, encumbrances, illegal construction)

Thorough due diligence — often with expert help — prevents future disputes and secures your home investment.
